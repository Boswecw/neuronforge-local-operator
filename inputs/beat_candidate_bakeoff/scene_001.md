# Scene 001 — The Door Under the Chapel

The rain had started before dusk and never really became weather. It became a presence.

By midnight it drummed against the slate roof of Saint Ardin’s chapel like a thousand impatient fingers, soft one moment, furious the next. Water crept down the old stained-glass windows in wavering lines, turning saints into ghosts. Inside, the chapel smelled of wet wool, candle smoke, and the old mineral cold of stone that had spent centuries forgetting the sun.

Mara Vey stood at the foot of the ruined altar, one hand on the iron key hanging at her throat, listening.

Not to the storm.

To the knocking.

Three slow impacts rose from somewhere below the floor. Not loud. Not dramatic. Deliberate.

Knock.

A pause long enough for doubt.

Knock.

Then the third, slightly weaker than the first two, as if whatever waited under the chapel was growing tired.

“Tell me you heard that,” said Tomas.

He stood two pews back with the lantern held too high, its yellow light flattening his face into sharp planes and hollows. Rain gleamed on his coat. He had not removed it since they’d entered, as if keeping it on might help him leave faster.

“I heard it,” Mara said.

Tomas swallowed. “That’s not possible.”

“Useful observation.”

“I’m serious.”

“So am I.”

He lowered the lantern a little. “This place was sealed.”

“That’s what the records said.”

“The records also said the reliquary was empty.”

Mara glanced toward the opened stone compartment behind the altar. Empty indeed—unless one counted the ring of black dust left behind, or the smell that had come out of it when she cracked the seal, something old and metallic, like coins kept too long in a dead man’s mouth.

The knocking came again.

This time there were only two strikes.

Tomas took an involuntary step backward. “Mara.”

She hated when he used her name like that—like it was a plea instead of a word.

Her fingers tightened around the key. The metal was warmer now than it should have been. When Father Ellion had pressed it into her palm six days earlier, he had been bleeding through the bandages around his ribs and too proud to sit down.

_If the chapel calls to you, don’t answer with prayer,_ he had said. _Answer with iron._

At the time she’d thought it was fever talking.

Now she wasn’t so sure.

“Bring the lantern,” she said.

“No.”

She turned and looked at him fully. Tomas was younger than she was by three years, but tonight he seemed younger by ten. His jaw worked once, twice. He had scholar’s hands, careful hands, the hands of a man more familiar with vellum than grave dirt. There was mud on those hands now.

“You volunteered for the archive retrieval,” Mara said.

“I volunteered for books. Manuscripts. Possibly a cursed inventory ledger.” He pointed toward the flagstones with a shaky finger. “Not whatever that is.”

Below them, silence spread out again, thick as held breath.

Mara crouched beside the altar steps and brushed her fingertips over the floor. The chapel stones were old enough that the seams between them had softened, but here—directly before the altar—one line remained unnaturally clean. A crescent. The edge of something fitted into the floor rather than built with it.

A door.

She slipped the key from her neck.

Tomas made a sound that was almost a laugh and not at all amused. “You are not opening that.”

“Why else would the lock exist?”

“Why else would someone bury it under a chapel?”

“That,” Mara said, “is precisely what I intend to find out.”

She inserted the iron key into a narrow slot hidden in the grout line. For an instant nothing happened. Then the key turned with a deep internal clunk that shivered up her wrist and into her teeth.

All the candles in the chapel went out.

Tomas cursed. The lantern guttered violently, then steadied, its flame reduced to a thin, stubborn blade.

Stone grated against stone.

The crescent seam widened into a black mouth in the floor, exhaling air so cold it smelled of extinguished fires and river caves. Not rot. Not tomb-stink. Something cleaner than death, which was somehow worse.

Mara stood slowly.

Steps descended into darkness.

“Close it,” Tomas whispered.

“Why?”

“Because there is a reason men with better judgment than us sealed that.”

Mara almost said _men with authority are rarely men with judgment_, but the words died before she gave them breath. Something had changed in the air. The silence below was no longer empty.

Something was listening back.

Then, from the dark beneath the chapel, a voice rose up—faint, rough, unmistakably human.

“Don’t let it wear my face.”

Tomas went white.

The lantern slipped in his grip and would have fallen if Mara hadn’t caught the handle. He stared at the open stair as though his mind had struck a wall and broken on it.

“That,” he said hoarsely, “was Captain Hale.”

Mara knew the name. Everyone in the expedition knew it. Captain Joren Hale had led the first descent into the drowned catacombs under Aram Thule eleven years ago. He and nine others had vanished. No bodies recovered. No journals found. Just one rope burned through as neatly as if by a hot blade.

Mara lifted the lantern. “You knew him?”

Tomas didn’t answer at once. Rain hammered the chapel roof overhead. Somewhere outside, thunder rolled over the hills, delayed and enormous.

Finally he said, “He was my brother.”

The admission struck her harder than the voice from below.

She looked at him again, really looked. Same dark eyes. Same cleft in the chin. She had seen Hale’s portrait in the Academy hall and somehow never made the connection. Tomas had avoided the subject with professional skill.

“You should have told me,” she said.

“And you would have done what? Left me behind?”

“Yes.”

A bitter, humorless smile cut across his face. “Exactly.”

Before Mara could answer, the voice came again.

Closer now.

“Tomas.”

He flinched like he’d been slapped.

“Mara,” he said, and there was terror in his voice now, stripped clean of embarrassment. “That is his voice.”

“I know.”

“No. I mean exactly.” His breath had gone shallow. “That cadence. The break on the second syllable. Nobody would know that.”

The lantern light quivered across the chapel walls. The saints in the stained glass looked away.

From below: “It learned me.”

A long silence followed.

Then, quietly, from somewhere deeper still, came the sound of many bare feet shifting on stone.

Mara felt every hair rise along her arms.

“All right,” she said, voice low and steady because one of them had to be steady. “New rule.”

Tomas tore his gaze from the stairs to look at her.

“If anything down there speaks in a voice we know, we assume it’s lying.”

“And if it isn’t?”

“Then we survive first and grieve later.”

He shut his eyes once, hard, then nodded.

Good enough.

Mara reached into her satchel and drew out the length of red chalk Father Ellion had insisted she carry. On the flagstone beside the opening, she marked a straight line from one edge of the stair mouth to the other, then crossed it with a second line and pressed her thumb into the center.

Tomas watched, breathing through his fear. “What is that supposed to do?”

“Make me feel like I’m doing something intelligent.”

“That is not comforting.”

“It wasn’t meant to be.”

Again the voice drifted upward, softer this time. Weaker.

“Please.”

Mara stared into the stairwell. The dark below seemed too complete, as if the lantern light did not enter it so much as get swallowed whole. She had descended wells, crypts, sinkholes, collapsed watchtowers, and once a nest of bone-white tunnels under the salt cliffs of Merrow Coast. Darkness was usually just darkness.

This felt occupied.

Behind her, wind shoved at the chapel doors with a booming thud. Tomas nearly jumped out of his skin.

Mara inhaled once, slow and deep, then handed him the satchel.

“If I start talking to someone who isn’t there,” she said, “hit me.”

“That seems inadequate.”

“Hit me hard.”

“That seems less inadequate.”

She almost smiled. Almost.

Then she started down the stairs.

The temperature dropped with each step. By the sixth, her breath showed pale in the lantern glow. By the tenth, the sounds of the storm above had dulled until the world seemed divided into two places: the living chapel overhead and this narrow throat of stone descending into whatever had been hidden beneath it.

The stair opened into a round chamber no bigger than a grain cellar.

Its walls were carved with figures worn nearly smooth by time: kneeling people with their hands over their mouths; a towering shape with no face, only a circle where the features should have been; and beneath that, a series of scratched lines, dense and frantic, as if someone had tried to erase the carvings with a knife and failed.

In the middle of the chamber sat a man bound to a chair of black wood.

The lantern shook in Mara’s grip.

Captain Joren Hale—older, gaunter, but recognizably the man from the Academy portrait—lifted his head against the light. His beard was threaded with gray. His cheeks were hollow. One eye was swollen nearly shut. Iron wire pinned his wrists to the chair arms so tightly that dried blood lacquered the wood beneath.

Tomas stopped dead on the stair behind her and made a sound like his soul had torn.

“Hale,” Mara said carefully.

The man in the chair stared at her with one bloodshot eye and began to laugh.

Not relief. Not hysteria. Contempt.

“You brought my brother,” he rasped. “That was kind of you.”

Mara’s skin went cold for an entirely different reason.

Slowly, very slowly, Captain Hale turned his gaze past her to Tomas.

And smiled with too many teeth.
