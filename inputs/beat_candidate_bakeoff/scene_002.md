# Scene 006 — What Was Left on the Table

By the time Lena unlocked the house, the sun was already down.

Not fully. There was still a residue of it in the west, a thin bruised band of purple behind the trees, but the porch and the front hall had gone gray. The kind of gray that made everything look paused rather than empty.

She stepped inside and stopped with the door still half open behind her.

The kitchen light was on.

For a second she thought only of electricity—of the bill, of the bulb she kept meaning to replace over the sink, of the fact that she distinctly remembered switching everything off before she left that morning. Then the smell reached her.

Coffee.

Fresh enough to still be warm in the air.

Lena closed the door quietly.

The house made no sound beyond the ordinary settling of old wood and the faint metallic tap of the loose gutter outside. She set her keys on the hall table, then picked them back up again without knowing why. The brass edge of the house key pressed into her palm.

“Mark?”

Nothing answered.

She did not call louder.

The kitchen sat at the back of the house with a narrow window over the sink and a table too large for one person, though it had been one person using it for almost eight months now. The overhead light cast its usual pale circle downward, flattening the room into familiar surfaces: chipped blue ceramic bowl by the fruit basket, stack of unopened mail, the chair with the split cane seat nobody ever fixed.

And on the table, beside her mug, sat a second cup.

Steam no longer rose from it, but there was still a dark crescent of wet heat on the wood beneath.

Lena stood in the doorway, coat still on.

Her own cup was the green one with the cracked handle. The other was white stoneware. Plain. Heavy. One of the old set they used when people came over, back when people still came over.

She looked at the coffee first, then at the chair pulled slightly away from the table.

Not dramatically. Just enough that someone had used it.

“Mark?” she said again, and this time the name came out flatter, less like a question than a test.

The refrigerator hummed. Somewhere upstairs a floorboard creaked.

She waited, but the house returned to stillness so completely that the creak began to feel like memory correcting itself after the fact.

Lena crossed the room and touched the handle of the white cup.

Warm.

Not hot. Not imagined. Warm.

She set her keys on the table and looked around with the careful, incremental focus of someone trying not to startle herself into foolishness. The back door was locked. The chain was still on. The window above the sink was latched from the inside. Nothing seemed disturbed. Nothing overturned, forced, rifled, broken. The mail was where she’d left it. The dish towel still hung crooked from the oven handle.

Her gaze moved to the counter.

The coffee tin was open.

Two spoons lay in the sink.

Not one.

Two.

Lena became aware, very suddenly, of her own breathing.

At the edge of the refrigerator door, held in place by a magnet shaped like a strawberry, was a folded piece of paper.

She knew before touching it that it was not there this morning.

The handwriting on the outside was familiar enough to send a small clean shock through her chest.

_Len._

Nothing else. Just that.

She did not open it right away.

Instead she looked toward the staircase visible from the kitchen archway. Darkness filled the upper hall. No movement. No voice. Just the suggestion of the house extending upward into itself.

She unfolded the note.

I stopped by.

That was all.

No signature.

No explanation.

No time.

No apology, which was somehow the loudest thing about it.

Lena read the sentence twice more as if another line might surface if she stared long enough.

I stopped by.

Her mouth went dry.

There were maybe six people in the world who still wrote her name that way, with the tail of the _L_ cutting too far under the next letter. Only one of them would have entered this house without knocking once upon a time. Only one of them brewed coffee too strong and never rinsed the spoon.

But Mark had been dead for seven months, three weeks, and two days.

She set the note down with great care.

Outside, tires whispered over wet road at the front of the house, then faded. A dog barked twice somewhere far off. The ordinary world remained offensively intact.

Lena took off her coat and draped it over the back of her chair without remembering doing it. Her fingers had begun to shake, but only a little. Enough to notice. Not enough to excuse.

She picked up the white cup and brought it closer.

Black coffee. No cream. Half a teaspoon of sugar, if that. Exactly the way Mark drank it after the doctor told him to cut back and he pretended that half-measures counted as virtue.

Her eyes drifted to the hall table.

Her keys were not there.

She looked down and saw them where she had left them on the kitchen table, next to the note.

She could not remember carrying them into the room.

Lena pressed the heel of one hand against her forehead.

“You’re tired,” she said aloud, and the sound of her own voice in the lit kitchen made her feel briefly less alone and then much more so.

Tired was true. She had spent the day at the firm correcting other people’s mistakes and the drive home in rain that came and went without ever deciding what season it belonged to. She had not been sleeping well. The doctor had said grief could do strange things to concentration, to memory, to appetite, to time.

Grief, however, generally did not make coffee for two.

Her gaze moved again to the second chair.

There was a dark coat hanging over the hook by the mudroom door.

Lena turned slowly.

It was not her coat.

For one absurd second she thought only that it looked too long in the sleeves. Then she knew it. Not by the coat itself—charcoal wool was charcoal wool—but by the small repair near the right pocket where she had once clumsily resewn the seam and Mark had teased her for making it look like battlefield surgery.

The coat hung still.

Rainwater had dried in faint dark patches along one shoulder.

Lena crossed to it as if sleepwalking and touched the fabric with two fingers.

Damp. Or not damp. Cool, at least.

One of the pockets sagged.

Something was inside.

She slid her hand in and drew out a small brass lighter.

Her throat tightened. The lighter was scratched on one side, the engraved initials nearly worn smooth. She had found it in a drawer three months after the funeral and put it in a box with the watch, the receipts, the loose change, the things that still seemed too shaped by him to throw away.

The box was upstairs in the bedroom closet.

Unless she had moved it.

Unless she had brought the lighter downstairs and forgotten.

Unless—

Upstairs, distinctly this time, something moved.

Not a creak. Not settling wood. A soft impact, followed by the faint drag of something against the floor.

Lena did not breathe.

The house held itself around her.

She looked toward the staircase and felt a ridiculous, immediate conviction that if she went up there, something irreversible would happen. Not necessarily bad. Not necessarily dangerous. Just final.

Her phone was in her bag by the front door. She could call someone. Nina, maybe. Or the police, though what exactly was she supposed to report? That her dead husband might be upstairs, unless grief had developed a taste for stagecraft?

Another soft sound.

This one from above and slightly to the left.

Bedroom side.

The lighter sat cold and solid in her palm.

“Mark,” she said, barely above a whisper now.

No answer.

Then, after a long pause, the bedroom light came on under the upstairs door, a thin line of gold appearing across the hall floor.

Lena stared at it.

She knew with absolute certainty that she had left that room dark.

A pulse began hammering in her throat.

There were explanations. There had to be explanations. Faulty wiring. A timer she forgot about. Someone in the house. Someone very much alive in the house, which was in many ways the less comforting possibility.

On the kitchen table, beside the note, her green mug had begun to drip.

Not from a crack. From condensation gathering under her fingers where she had touched it.

No.

Not condensation.

The mug was warm too.

Lena backed one step toward the counter without meaning to.

She looked from the mug to the staircase to the coat on the hook.

At the edge of her vision, beyond the reflection in the dark kitchen window, someone crossed the upstairs hall.

Just a passing shape, gone before she could turn.

Tall.

Familiar in the way familiar things become unbearable.

The bedroom light went out.

Everything in the house seemed to listen.

Lena stood in the kitchen with the lighter in one hand and the note in the other, not yet willing to go upstairs, no longer willing to stay where she was, while the coffee on the table slowly gave up its heat.
