Tariq found the letter on the kitchen table, propped against the sugar bowl as though it had been left by someone expecting him back. The envelope was unsealed. He stood in the doorway a moment, coat still on, the door swinging shut behind him.

He recognized the handwriting before he'd fully crossed the room. His sister's looping, deliberate script — the kind of handwriting formed by someone who had always written as though the words might be graded. He sat down at the table. He did not open the envelope immediately.

Outside, the street was doing what it usually did at this hour: a bin lorry working its way down the terrace, a dog barking two houses along, the low wash of radio from the upstairs flat. All of it filtered down to Tariq as background texture, present but unimportant.

He turned the envelope over. The flap was unsealed, the paper slightly wrinkled at one corner, as if it had been handled several times before being left here. He slid out the single page and read it in full, then set it face-down on the table and looked at the ceiling for a long moment.

Then he got up, put the kettle on, and started over from the beginning.
