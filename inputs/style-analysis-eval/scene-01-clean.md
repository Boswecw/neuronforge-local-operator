The morning came in cold and quiet. Saren pulled on her coat before she crossed the yard, the frost still unbroken in the corners where the sun hadn't reached yet. The well chain rang once when she hauled it up, a clean sound that carried further than it should have in that stillness.

Across the field, the tree line stood dark against a pale sky. She watched it for a moment. Nothing moved, and that was fine. The birds had come back last week, and the silence now was ordinary silence, not the hollow kind she'd known in the bad months.

She set the bucket on the stone lip and looked at her reflection in the water. It looked back, dim and wavering.

"You'll want to eat before you go," her mother called from the doorway.

Saren didn't turn. "I know."

"Knowing and doing aren't the same."

That was true enough. Saren picked up the bucket and started back toward the house. The grass bent under her boots, still soft from the thaw. She thought about the road north and how long it would take and what she would find at the end of it, and none of those thoughts were pleasant, but she carried them the way you carry a stone in your boot — not without noticing, not without wanting it gone, but walking all the same.

Her mother had already gone back inside by the time Saren reached the step. The smell of porridge and woodsmoke pushed out through the open door.

She went in.
