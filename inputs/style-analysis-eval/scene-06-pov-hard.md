Detective Reyes stood at the edge of the crime scene tape and studied the body. The rain had washed most of the blood toward the gutter, leaving a faint pink trail between the cobblestones. She pulled on a latex glove and crouched beside the victim. The man was face-down, expensive suit, no wallet, one shoe missing.

She photographed what she needed and rose. Her partner, Ortega, was interviewing the woman who'd called it in — an elderly neighbor clutching a terrier to her chest as though it might absorb the shock. Reyes could read the interview from twenty feet away: the woman had heard nothing unusual, had only come outside because the dog needed walking, had nearly tripped over the body in the dark.

I know what you're thinking. You're wondering why she didn't call out to the man — whether he might have been saved if someone had checked sooner. Reyes would have wondered the same thing. But sometimes the window closes before anyone knows it was open, and no amount of replaying the night changes that.

Reyes walked back to the car. She pulled her notebook and wrote three words: no watch either. Whatever had happened here, someone had been thorough. She didn't like thorough. Thorough meant planned. Planned meant they'd do it again.
